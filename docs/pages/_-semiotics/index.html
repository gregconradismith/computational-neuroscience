_ Semiotics

Preparation for this class

Read ... Grush, R., 2001. The semantic challenge to computational neuroscience. Theory and Method in the Neurosciences, pp.155-172.     Semiotics is the "study of signs." 
A sign is traditionally defined as "something which stands for something else." ... All meaningful phenomena (including words and images) are signs. To interpret something is to treat it as a sign.  All experience is mediated by signs, and communication depends on them.  Semioticians study how meanings are made and how reality is represented (and indeed constructed) through signs and sign systems. (Chandler  2007.  Semiotics: the basics. p. 2)
 
Exploring semiotic perspectives helps us to realize that information or meaning is not "contained" in the world, or in books, computers, or other media. Meaning is not "transmitted" to us -- we actively interpret texts and the world according to a complex interplay of frames of reference.  Semiotics helps us take apart what was taken for granted, making our interpretive systems more explicit.(Chandler  2007.  Semiotics: the basics. p. 8)
 
In defining realities, sign systems serve ideological functions. Deconstructing and contesting realities they represent can reveal whose realities are privileged and whose are suppressed.  Such a study involves investigating the construction and maintenance of reality by particular social groups.  To decline the study of signs is to leave to others the control of the world of meanings that we inhabit. (Chandler  2007.  Semiotics: the basics. p. 9)
