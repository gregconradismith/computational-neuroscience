The following material is drawn from the appendix of <em>More Data, More Noise:  A celebration of the 60th birthday of Jerome Y. Lettvin. </em>

In 1973, Jerry recorded an Interactive Lecture entitled  "A View on the Function of a Neuron.''  It is an introduction to neurophysiology which at the same time gets right into the issues of the mind-brain problem.  Jerry answers 31 questions in connection with the lecture. The following two answers, from toward the end of the lecture, seem to me to be especially memorable and inspiring.

[The author of <em>More Data, More Noise</em> transcribed in a way that allowed others to hear Jerome Lettvin's voice.  I have edited - sometimes paraphrased - Lettvin's answers.]

<strong>What are the principal difficulties in doing nervous system experiments?</strong>

The principal difficulty in doing nervous system experiments is imagining what sort of experiments to do.  Surprisingly, most physiological experiments are not difficult.  In fact, one can easily train high school and grammar school students to do the technical work.

The really important part of nervous systems experiments is not the microelectrodes and the oscilloscopes, the flashing lights and the meters, or the people in white coats looking terribly intense and antiseptic.  The important thing is what you are trying to find out about the system -- and the principal difficulty in doing nervous system experiments is deciding what the results are going to <em>mean</em>.

You can decide, of course, to work on the brain as a tissue, in which case you are going to examine tissue properties, and that's one of the great things that Hodgkin and Huxley did. They described tissue properties of nerve membranes, cell properties, and physiology. That's OK, that's perfectly legitimate. But that is <em>not</em> a nervous system experiment; it's a cell-level experiment. ...

You have to realize that when you're working with the nervous system you're not dealing with ordinary measurables, or energy, you're dealing with information. Information as a substance is very different from the stuff materialists talk about.  I'm not saying that you have to become a mystic, but you must pursue some lawfulness for the system.   I would say that the fundamental difficulty in doing nervous system experiments is thinking up experiments that are meaningful in anything except a trivial way.

I can, for example, go and measure knee jerks. And, you know, how much the muscle contracts for each stimulus I give the nerve ... I can do this many times.  I can show that with a full bladder, the knee jerk is inhibited, and with certain times of the estrous cycle ... the knee jerk is enhanced. I can do all of this, but in the end, I don't really <i>have </i>much to talk about.  And if I keep on handling the different parts of the nervous system as if they were extended knee jerks, then in the end, all I have is a collection of volumes of knee jerks and no way of putting them together.  So, the principal difficulties in doing nervous system research are conceptual.

<strong>What would you like to do next?</strong>

What has been evident throughout this lecture, and the questions that this lecture has occasioned, is <em>a kind of impalpability of this whole business</em>.  If I were a student, I don't know if I'd be satisfied with the answers to the questions.  Certainly, being the lecturer, I’m not satisfied with the lectures or with the answers I’ve given to the questions.

What is it that I've planned to do?  What sort of work is in the offing?  The answer, I suppose, would be to find a way of putting these questions seriously.  That is, there must be a right way of asking questions about information and information handling that does not boil down to the, shall we say, the elementary and very special case of information as it is presently handled in [Shannon's] <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory">information theory</a>.

I'm not trying to say there must be some deep mystical quality of information not handled by numbers.  No, all I'm saying is that, for the most part, the kinds of information that we deal with nervous systems experiments are not accounted for by the kinds of information that electrical engineers dump into their wires.  The ways of combining information in the nervous system are very different from ways of combining information in the context of computers.  It's not that a computer can not be a brain, or a brain can't be a computer.  I'm not saying anything of the sort.  All I am saying is that computers and brains are very different types of systems, and it's not obvious to me that one can go from one to the other.

I suppose that what I want to do most of all right now is find a way of re-introducing semantical problems in a way that finally asks questions about the relationship of the message to the content of the message. Not the sign and the thing signified, I think that's the wrong way of putting it, but the <em>message and the meaning of the message</em>.  I'm not sure that this is decomposable into the sorts of things that people have previously decomposed messages into, you know, the sets of signs or sets of signals and that which is signified by the signals, because we have seen that if information is distributed, that which is signified has no meaning in a distributed system. There must be a way of handling the idea of information in a distributed system that makes sense.

I suppose I'll devote my remaining years to trying to make sense of it. I doubt very much if I will, and it may be that I'm on the wrong track. At least some of my students are going to try this. Other people will try other things.

But make no bones about it, the crucial and most important scientific work that one has to do now lies not in the material aspects of the world, namely, how do you combine compound A and B, what are the different modes of combination.  Rather, <em>the crucial work lies in what you might call the informational aspects of the world.</em>  These have <em>never </em>been adequately explored; rather, they have been neglected as a kind of informal consequence of energetic processes.

But, more and more, as we proceed into the world of complex modern science, it becomes obvious that the cheerful semi-determinism of most of the biophysicists is really uncalled for and certainly has no place with respect to the nervous system.
