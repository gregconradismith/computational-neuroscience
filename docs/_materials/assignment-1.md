While home for Spring Break, walking the dog with one of your parents, you are introduced to a high school science teacher who recently moved into the neighborhood.   You talk, with dogs on leash, and it becomes apparent that Ms. Z is an experienced teacher midway in age between yourself and your parents.    She is delighted to meet you, a William &amp; Mary student with knowledge of neuroscience, because she has been entertaining the idea of including more neuroscience in the curriculum of her biology course.  Your parent invites Ms. Z over for dinner on that very evening.

After some home cooked food and casual chit-chat, your parents instigate the continuation of this conversation with Ms. Z about neuroscience.   She admits that it is the computational aspects of neuroscience that are her concern.

This makes sense.  Ms. Z is a UVA graduate with a degree in biology.  In fact, she considered herself to be a pre-medical student until her junior year.  (She tells the story of a disappointing and eye-opening experience as a scribe for a local physician whose practice was under extreme financial pressure.)   "Anyways, the point is," says Ms. Z, "that I am intellectually quite comfortable with neurobiology, physiological psychology, biochemistry, and so on.  What I am having  difficultly understanding is the information processing language that contemporary neuroscientists are using in discussions of the function of the brain."

In an animated, intelligent-yet-perplexed manner, she continues, "Is this `information processing' talk just a metaphor?  Is it a scientific hypothesis?  Is it a philosophical claim?"  Then, looking directly at you, she asks "Do you really believe that the brain is a computer?"

At that very moment your cell phone rings.   The caller is a cherished high school friend in need.  Everyone at the dinner table agrees that the computational neuroscience conversation must be put on hold so that you can provide assistance to your friend.  You are, as they say, saved by the bell.

But when you return home your parents explain that Ms. Z remains eager to hear your answer to her questions.   Your parent gives you her email address and suggests that <strong>an email of about</strong> <strong>1200 words should suffice</strong>.

With a smirk, your parent says that this is a great opportunity to show how your liberal arts education has equipped you as a critical thinker.  By this they mean that you are able to write well, clearly describe key concepts, and present a s<span lang="RU">pecific position that is imaginative and yet takes into account the issues complexities, uses information from germane and reliable sources, analyzes assumptions and expert viewpoints, and s</span>ynthesizes others' points of view within your own position.

Says your beloved parent:  "She seems mostly interested in your professional opinion about this `Is the brain a computer?' question. But if you have any ideas about how to introduce the relevant concepts and issues to her 10th grade biology class, I'm sure she'd really appreciate that."
