<h2 class="wp-block-heading">What is Computational Neuroscience?</h2>


<p><strong>Computational Neuroscience</strong> has two facets:</p>


<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Development of realistic computational models of neural phenomena, and</li>


<li>Understanding how nervous systems compute and process information.</li>
</ul>


<p>Our focus is this second facet: <em>Computational neuroscience as the study of how nervous systems perform computations and process information</em>.  A major theme of this course will be the <strong>three levels of inquiry</strong> suggested by the visual neuroscientist <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Marr_(neuroscientist)">David Marr</a>:</p>


<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Computational</strong>: What computations does the central nervous system perform and why?</li>


<li><strong>Algorithmic</strong>: What representations and procedures are used in the neural computation?</li>


<li><strong>Implementation</strong>: What the physiological mechanisms that bring about these representations and carry out these algorithms?</li>
</ul>


<p>We will use Marr's framework to explore the computational function of the brain - hindbrain, hippocampus, thalamus, basal ganglia, and visual cortex - emphasizing how <strong>theory</strong> complements <strong>experiment</strong> in <strong>systems neuroscience</strong>.</p>


<p>Prerequisites: <em>APSC 351 Cellular Biophysics and Modeling</em> or permission of instructor.</p>


<h2 class="wp-block-heading">What to expect</h2>


<p>This is a seminar-style course with readings from scientific literature, discussion and critical analysis, lecture, and student projects and presentations.</p>


<p>The intended audience is neuroscience majors who are interested in computational and systems neuroscience, but may not have formal training in mathematics and computational methods.</p>


<p>There are no required textbook purchases.   <a href="../readings/">Readings</a> will be drawn from the primary scientific literature will be supplemented by select passages from <a href="https://a.co/iJop5lM">computational neuroscience textbooks</a>.</p>


<h2 class="wp-block-heading">COLL 400 capstone learning expectations</h2>


<p>The <a href="https://www.wm.edu/as/facultyresources/committees/educationalpolicy/coll-curriculum/400/index.php">COLL 400</a> capstone experience requires students to take initiative in synthesis and critical analysis, to solve problems in an applied and/or academic setting, to create original material or original scholarship, and to communicate effectively with a diversity of audiences.</p>


<p>We will achieve these goals together through a variety of in-class and out-of-class assignments.  Please use your best handwriting for in-class assignments.  Out-of-class assignments must be typeset (not handwritten).  The one exception is your scientific notebook (see below).</p>


<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Evaluation and projects</h2>


<p>A serious engagement with the lectures, readings, videos, and assigned problems is absolutely required.  This will be assessed as follows.</p>


<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Participation and professionalism (15%)</strong></h3>


<p>This is a discussion course. Attendance is required.  You are expected to listen and speak.  Email me in advance if you are going to miss class. This can happen up to 2 times in the semester without negatively affecting your participation grade.   If you are managing long term illnesses or other circumstance that could require missing more than 2 classes, you must work with the Dean of Students Office to provide documentation.</p>


<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Scientific notebook  (15%)</strong></h3>


<p>Bring your Notebook and relevant readings to class.  Questions, observations and notes written in response to required readings must be completed prior to each class.</p>


<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Assignments and Quizzes (15%) </h3>


<p>There will occasionally be unannounced in-class assignments and/or quizzes. For example, easy questions meant to determine whether or not the readings are being completed. </p>


<p>If asked to upload materials in preparation for class, this should occur by the end of the previous day (to allow time for the instructor or other students to review prior to class).  That is, if an activity is assigned that is needed for discussion during class on Tuesday, then upload by 11:59pm Monday. If needed for class Thursday, then upload by 11:59pm Wednesday.</p>


<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Midterm project (15%)</h3>


<p>The midterm project will be a critical review of a suggested reading related to the course objectives.  Book choice will occur about 1/4 of the way through the semester.  Due date will be about 1/2 way through the semester.</p>


<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Term project (40%)</strong></h3>


<p>Your term project will involve independent library research and deep intellectual engagement with a computational aspect of central nervous system function of interest to you.</p>


<p>The project will be evaluated through</p>


<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>a preliminary written project proposal (5% of final grade),</li>


<li>an annotated bibliography and oral progress report (10%), and</li>


<li>a final deliverable (25%).</li>
</ul>


<p>Project proposals will be due shortly after the midterm project and must be approved by the instructor. The oral progress report will occur about 3/4 of the way through the semester. The final written report should be completed before final exam week ends.</p>


<p>Students are always evaluated with reference to course objectives, never by relative performance.</p>
